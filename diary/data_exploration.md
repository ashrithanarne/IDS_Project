## **Data Exploration**

Exploratory analysis focused on patterns of media coverage across crises and how reporting intensity relates to crisis-specific indicators. Using metrics such as **total coverage**, **coverage per day**, and **coverage per funding/person affected**, we observed substantial variation in attention across crises, indicating that media focus is not uniformly distributed.

- **Crisis-level insights:** 
  - Gaza and Ukraine received disproportionately high coverage relative to other crises. 
  - Some long-standing crises like Syria and Yemen maintained steady attention, while others, such as Chad or Uganda, had consistently low coverage.  
- **Temporal patterns:** 
  - Monthly coverage trends revealed spikes in reporting around the onset of new crises and episodic attention rather than steady monitoring across crisis duration.  
- **Outlet-level variation:** 
  - **Al Jazeera** dominates total coverage, significantly more than **Reuters**.  
 - **Reuters** slightly exceeds **The New York Times**, which ranks third.  
 - There is a minor gap between the third and fourth-ranked outlet, while the coverage gradually declines from there.  
 - Overall, media coverage is not heavily concentrated in the top few outlets, but instead shows a steady decline across the rest of the outlets.  
- **Entity and framing-level insights:** 
  - Certain entities (e.g., Hamas, Zelensky) are portrayed with consistent sentiment patterns.  
  - Humanitarian framing dominates article classification, while legal framing is comparatively rare.

These exploratory observations highlight **discrepancies between media attention and measurable crisis severity**, suggesting that factors beyond humanitarian magnitude—such as narrative framing, outlet preference, or political salience—may influence coverage.
